## Summary Assessment

**Overall recommendation:** Accept with Minor Revision (Score: 4.0/5)

This paper presents a unified EDL+ORCU framework for dental fluorosis diagnosis that addresses three genuine gaps in the literature: ordinality, uncertainty quantification, and calibration. The shared-logit architecture is elegant—a single evidence head serves dual purposes without requiring separate prediction heads. The five-loss systematic comparison under a controlled ViT-Base backbone is methodologically rigorous and provides practical guidance for loss selection in small-sample medical imaging.

The paper's key strengths are: (1) a clean architectural contribution (shared-logit bridge) that is backbone-agnostic and applicable beyond fluorosis; (2) the multi-seed stability finding—EDL's CV of 4.3% vs CE's 31.4% is striking and has implications for any small-sample medical deployment; (3) honest reporting of negative results (EDL+ORCU underperformance on single splits, AUROC($u$) < 0.5); and (4) practical clinical triage workflow grounded in real uncertainty thresholds.

The primary concerns are moderate: (1) version inconsistency between main results and per-class analysis (v2.2 vs v6), (2) single-center dataset limits generalizability claims, (3) clinical triage is not prospectively validated, (4) the EDL+ORCU tension is identified but not resolved, and (5) FusionDentNet bib entry has a likely incorrect arXiv ID. None are fatal to the core contribution.

### Strengths

1. **Shared-logit architecture is genuinely novel and well-motivated.** The evidence head outputs logits $z$ that serve both EDL (via softplus $\to \alpha$) and ORCU (via softmax $\to p$) paths—Section 3.2 explains this cleanly with equations (1)-(3). This avoids separate prediction heads and ensures consistency between evidence accumulation and ordinal constraints.

2. **Honest reporting of negative and null results.** The paper does not cherry-pick: EDL+ORCU underperforms EDL on single splits (70.00% vs 83.33%, Table 1); AUROC($u$) is below 0.5 for per-sample error detection; the overconfident misclassification example (Ex. C, $u=0.465$) is prominently featured. This transparency builds credibility.

3. **Multi-seed stability is a practically important finding.** The collapse of CE at seed 456 (35% accuracy, only 10pp above random) while EDL maintains 75% (Table 3) is striking. The KL regularization acting as implicit initialization stabilizer is a novel insight with implications beyond fluorosis.

4. **Five-loss systematic comparison under controlled backbone.** Comparing CE, Cumulative, SORD, EDL, and EDL+ORCU with identical ViT-Base pretraining provides the field with practical guidance on loss function selection for ordinal medical imaging. This fills a genuine methodological gap.

5. **Clinical triage workflow is concrete and actionable.** Figure 15 and the theta sweep (Figure 14) translate uncertainty quantification into a practical decision-support system with specific operating points ($\theta = 0.30$).

- **Dimension:** Experimental Rigor

Section 5.1 states "v2.2 data source (PyTorch 2.5.1)" for the main table, but Section 5.6 reports per-class metrics from v6. ### Reviewer Objections

#### RO1: "How do we know the EDL improvement is not just better regularization from the KL term rather than anything specific to evidential learning?"
**Why it matters:** If the 1.67pp gain over CE is attributable to KL regularization rather than Dirichlet parameterization per se, then the contribution is a regularization technique, not an uncertainty quantification framework. The claim that uncertainty is "calibrated Dirichlet uncertainty" would be misleading if the uncertainty values don't actually separate correct from incorrect predictions.
**How to address it:** Add a baseline: CE + label smoothing (which has a similar regularizing effect to the KL term) or CE + standard L2 regularization tuned to match EDL's accuracy. If EDL's uncertainty metrics (ECE, SafePred) still outperform this baseline, the Dirichlet parameterization is doing more than regularization. If not, the contribution should be reframed.

#### RO2: "The AUROC($u$) < 0.5 result means the uncertainty is anti-informative for individual decisions. How can you then claim it's useful for clinical triage?"
**Why it matters:** Clinical triage operates on individual patients—if $u$ cannot identify which specific samples are likely wrong, how does the triage system know which samples to flag? The theta sweep (Figure 14) shows population-level trends, but a clinician needs per-sample reliability.
**How to address it:** Show the distribution of correctness at each uncertainty level (a calibration-like plot but for uncertainty bins), not just aggregate SafePred. Distinguish between "uncertainty identifies a population of higher-risk samples" (which aggregate metrics support) vs "uncertainty identifies this specific sample as high-risk" (which AUROC tests). Add a per-bin analysis showing that while individual discrimination is poor, samples with $u > 0.6$ have measurably lower accuracy than samples with $u < 0.3$.

#### RO3: "Why not compare against a simple ensemble baseline for uncertainty?"
**Why it matters:** Deep ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017) are a strong baseline for uncertainty quantification. The paper cites ensembles but never compares against them. If 2-3 independently trained CE models achieve similar ECE to EDL through averaging, the case for EDL's uncertainty benefits would be weaker.
**How to address it:** Either (a) add a 3-ensemble CE baseline (even with only 3 seeds from Table 3, ensemble the predictions and compute ECE/SafePred), or (b) explicitly state that ensembles are orthogonal and can be combined with EDL, and that the contribution is single-model uncertainty without the 3$\times$ inference cost.

#### RO4: "The EDL+ORCU result is effectively negative. Is the combined method actually useful?"
**Why it matters:** EDL+ORCU underperforms EDL on every single-split metric (Table 1): accuracy 70.00% vs 83.33%, ECE 0.2524 vs 0.0719. The only positive is CV stability (Table 4). A method that loses 13pp accuracy for better CV stability is unlikely to be adopted. The paper's title and abstract feature EDL+ORCU prominently, but the results favor EDL alone.
**How to address it:** Consider reframing the paper to focus on EDL with ordinal awareness, and position ORCU as an extension that needs further development (e.g., decoupled optimization, Dirichlet-space ordinal prior). The current framing over-promises on the combined method.

#### RO5: "You claim SOTA but your CE baseline already beats all prior work. Is the 1.67pp gain from EDL over CE meaningful, or is the backbone doing all the work?"
**Why it matters:** If CE with ViT-Base/ImageNet-21k already achieves 81.67% (beating MLTrMR's 80.19%), then the true SOTA driver is the backbone choice, not the EDL method. The 1.67pp gain from EDL is modest and may not be statistically significant on 60 test samples.
**How to address it:** Report a statistical significance test (e.g., McNemar's test or bootstrap confidence intervals) for the 81.67% vs 83.33% difference. With n=60, a 1-sample difference (1/60 = 1.67pp) is unlikely to be significant. If not significant, soften the "new SOTA" claim to "competitive with SOTA while additionally providing calibrated uncertainty."

## Devil's Advocate Report

### Strongest Counter-Argument

The paper's central claim—that EDL provides meaningful uncertainty quantification for fluorosis diagnosis—rests on an unstable empirical foundation. The evidence for this claim is undermined by four specific issues:

First, the paper's own data shows that EDL uncertainty cannot identify individual misclassified samples (AUROC < 0.5, Section 6.3). A clinician receiving an individual diagnosis needs to know whether *this specific prediction* is reliable. The paper acknowledges this limitation but argues that population-level metrics (ECE, SafePred) are sufficient. This is a category error: calibration measures whether confidence matches accuracy *in aggregate*, not whether *this specific* high-confidence prediction is correct. A perfectly calibrated model can still be confidently wrong on any given sample.

Second, the version inconsistency between Tables 1 and 5 undermines confidence in the reported numbers. The main results (v2.2, PyTorch 2.5.1) show CE at 81.67% and EDL at 83.33%, but the per-class analysis (v6, PyTorch 2.6.0) reports different metrics. The multi-seed table shows CE at seed 42 = 61.7%, which contradicts the main CE result at the same seed (81.67%). Either the multi-seed table uses v6 data (not stated) or there is a 20pp discrepancy in the same experimental condition. Without clarification, a skeptical reviewer cannot trust the reported numbers.

Third, the clinical triage system is validated with 3 hand-picked examples. This is not a validation—it is an illustration. The theta sweep (Figure 14) provides population-level analysis, but the example reports show what the authors want to show (one success, one borderline, one failure). A skeptic would ask: what do the *worst* 10 examples look like? How many "Ex. C" (confidently wrong) cases are there in the test set? The paper does not report this.

Fourth, the EDL+ORCU result—featured in the title—is effectively negative. On every single-split metric, EDL+ORCU is worse than CE (70.00% vs 81.67% accuracy, 0.2524 vs 0.1213 ECE). The paper's response is that ORCU improves CV stability, but a 13pp accuracy loss for 0.36% QWK CV is a poor trade-off. If the combined method doesn't work, the title and abstract should not feature it so prominently.

Taken together, these issues suggest that EDL provides a modest regularization benefit (the multi-seed stability is the strongest finding) but does not provide clinically actionable per-sample uncertainty. The calibration improvement (ECE 0.0719) is real but orthogonal to the claimed clinical triage benefit. A more honest framing would be: "EDL provides implicit regularization that improves small-sample stability and calibration, with preliminary evidence that population-level uncertainty metrics could inform triage workflows."
