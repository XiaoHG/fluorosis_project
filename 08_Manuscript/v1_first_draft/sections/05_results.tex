\section{Results}

We present results on the DFID dataset (200 images, ViT-Base backbone). All primary experiments use v2.2 (PyTorch 2.5.1, correct model architecture with StandardClassifier for CE/SORD and EDLClassifier for EDL/EDL+ORCU). Per-sample prediction data exported from v6 (PyTorch 2.6.0) is used for visualization only.

\subsection{Main Results: Five-Mode Comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:df_main} reports the primary experiment comparing five loss modes on the DF test set (n=60). The same ViT-Base backbone and Evidence Head architecture are used for all modes; only the loss function and classifier head type differ.

CE, the standard baseline, achieves 81.67\% accuracy and 0.9329 QWK---already surpassing all published SOTA on this dataset. This reflects the strength of ImageNet-21k pretrained ViT-Base as a backbone for medical image classification.

\label{tab:df_main}
\begin{tabular}{lccccc}

\textbf{EDL achieves the best performance across all metrics:} 83.33\% accuracy (+1.67pp over CE), 0.8278 macro-F1, 0.9376 QWK, and critically, the lowest ECE at 0.0719---a 40.7\% reduction in calibration error compared to CE's 0.1213. EDL also maintains 96.7\% unimodal predictions, confirming that Dirichlet parameterization naturally produces concentrated probability distributions.

\textbf{EDL+ORCU underperforms expectations.} At 70.00\% accuracy with ECE 0.2524, the combined loss does not improve upon EDL alone on this dataset. We hypothesize two contributing factors: (1) the log-barrier regularization term directly competes with the KL regularizer in EDL, potentially over-constraining the logit space; and (2) with only 120 training samples, the joint optimization landscape may be too constrained. However, EDL+ORCU maintains 100\% unimodal predictions and shows the best cross-validation stability (see \S\ref{sec:cv}), suggesting the ordinal constraints provide regularization benefits even when single-run accuracy is lower.

\textbf{Cumulative loss produces unstable results} (68.33\% Acc, 91.7\% unimodal), consistent with its known sensitivity to threshold boundaries in small-sample settings.

\textbf{SORD provides a balanced trade-off:} 73.33\% accuracy with 100\% unimodal predictions and moderate calibration (ECE 0.1665), making it a safe clinical option when ordinal guarantees are prioritized over raw accuracy.

\subsection{Confusion Matrices and Reliability}

Figure~\ref{fig:f1} shows confusion matrices for all five modes. CE and EDL exhibit strong diagonal dominance. EDL+ORCU shows more off-diagonal scatter, particularly between adjacent classes (Mild $\leftrightarrow$ Moderate), consistent with ordinal constraint enforcement.

Figure~\ref{fig:f2} presents reliability diagrams with calibration gap ribbons. EDL (ECE 0.0719) shows the tightest alignment between confidence and accuracy across all bins. CE overestimates confidence in mid-range bins (ECE 0.1213), while EDL+ORCU shows systematic underconfidence (ECE 0.2524), suggesting the ordinal regularization penalizes confident predictions.

\subsection{Uncertainty Analysis}

Figure~\ref{fig:f3} visualizes uncertainty distributions across modes. EDL produces a wider spread of uncertainty values compared to the entropy-based uncertainty from CE, enabling finer discrimination between confident and uncertain predictions.

Figure~\ref{fig:f4} analyzes evidence distributions per class for EDL. The evidence head accumulates strongest evidence for Normal and Severe classes, with Moderate showing the lowest evidence levels---consistent with the greater feature overlap between Mild and Moderate fluorosis.

\subsection{Clinical Decision Support}

\subsubsection{Prediction Distribution Analysis}

Figure~\ref{fig:f8} shows the distribution of predicted probabilities across modes using ridge density plots and donut charts of unimodality. CE and SORD achieve 100\% unimodal predictions, while EDL achieves 96.7\% unimodality with 3.3\% of predictions exhibiting minor secondary modes (typically between adjacent classes), which is clinically interpretable.

\subsubsection{Auto-Diagnosis Theta Sweep}

Figure~\ref{fig:f14} presents the theta threshold sweep analysis, where $\theta$ defines the uncertainty threshold for automated versus manual review decisions. As $\theta$ increases, more samples are classified as ``safe'' for auto-diagnosis, but at the cost of including higher-uncertainty samples. EDL's SafePred (accuracy among low-uncertainty samples) remains above 0.85 for $\theta < 0.4$, while CE's SafePred degrades more rapidly.

At the recommended operating point $\theta = 0.30$ for EDL:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Auto-diagnosis rate:} $\sim$70\% of test samples eligible for automated reporting
    \item \textbf{SafePred:} $\sim$0.90 accuracy among auto-reported samples
    \item \textbf{RecAcc:} $\sim$0.85 (85\% of high-uncertainty samples correctly flagged for review)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Clinical Triage System}

Figure~\ref{fig:f15} illustrates the proposed clinical triage workflow. Test samples with EDL uncertainty $u < \theta$ proceed to automated diagnosis report generation; samples with $u \geq \theta$ are flagged for expert radiologist review. This uncertainty-gated triage provides a practical mechanism for deploying AI-assisted fluorosis screening in resource-limited settings while maintaining clinical safety through selective expert oversight.

\subsubsection{Example Diagnostic Reports}

Figure~\ref{fig:f16} presents three representative diagnostic report examples from the DFID test set:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Example A (Confident Auto-Diagnosis):} Sample \#6, True=Normal, Pred=Normal, confidence=0.998, $u=0.479 < \theta$. Correct prediction with low uncertainty---safe for auto-diagnosis.
    \item \textbf{Example B (Borderline---Needs Review):} Sample \#8, True=Mild, Pred=Mild, confidence=0.482, $u=0.647 \geq \theta$. The model is correct but highly uncertain (probabilities nearly evenly split between Mild and Moderate). Correctly flagged for manual review.
    \item \textbf{Example C (Dangerous Overconfident):} Sample \#7, True=Moderate, Pred=Mild, confidence=0.998, $u=0.465 < \theta$. The model is confidently wrong---this represents the most dangerous failure mode for automated diagnosis. While EDL uncertainty is moderately elevated for this sample, it falls below the decision threshold, highlighting the clinical risk of overconfident misclassifications and the need for conservative threshold selection with prospective validation.
\end{enumerate}

These examples illustrate both the promise and the limitations of uncertainty-based triage: EDL uncertainty generally identifies difficult cases, but overconfident misclassifications remain a clinical risk requiring careful threshold calibration.

\subsection{Summary of Key Findings}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{CE baseline (81.67\%) already exceeds all published SOTA} on DFID, demonstrating the effectiveness of ImageNet-21k pretrained ViT for medical image classification with small datasets.
    \item \textbf{EDL achieves new SOTA} at 83.33\% accuracy (+1.67pp over CE, +3.14pp over MLTrMR) with the lowest calibration error (ECE 0.0719).
    \item \textbf{EDL provides substantially better seed robustness} than CE (CV 4.3\% vs 31.4\%), with KL regularization acting as an implicit initialization stabilizer---critical for small-sample medical imaging.
    \item \textbf{EDL+ORCU provides the best cross-validation stability} (QWK std 0.0032, CV 0.36\%), confirming ordinal constraints improve generalization.
    \item \textbf{EDL uncertainty enables clinical triage,} but overconfident misclassifications remain a risk requiring careful threshold selection and prospective validation.
\end{enumerate}
